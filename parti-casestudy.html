Parti

Mobile App Design Case Study

Parti / D.O.T. / ASF

This mobile app case study was constructed to help get a better understanding of the UX/UI design process through a 5 step process: Research, Define, Ideate, Prototype, and Test. Working with a group to form a seemingly new app service idea, we collaborated and worked individually throughout the entire process.

My Role:

UI Designer - I worked with three colleagues and was involved throughout the entire process, but led the Prototpe and Testing phases.

Research

Proto-Persona

To begin our research process, we first wanted to learn more about our primary user. We brainstormed together to come up with different characteristics, wants, and needs. Defining this persona before gathering user research was beneficial in creating quality interview questions for the people we wanted to get insight from.

We settled on Valerie who is a busy mother that enjoys throwing parties, but doesn’t really have the time to spend on the preparation and clean-up.

User Insights & Affinity Diagram

We then created a plan and interviewed 5 potential users in order to obtain some qualitative feedback. We also created a survey that we shared on social media so we could gain quantitative feedback.

We sorted our data based on natural themes and discovered these things about our users:

  • Very busy
  • Host parties often
  • Hire help often
  • Like to socialize and look “put together”
  • Dislike driving around sourcing supplies
  • Dislike cleaning up
  • Most hire catering

Definition

User Scenario

Using the data that we gathered from our research, we iterated on our Proto-Persona to define our User Persona. While not much changed, the research was very helpful in confirming our hypothesis about our user.

This moved us into the Definition phase, where we added more specific informaiton to our Proto-Persona to create a User Scenario.

We focused on the simple journey of our user, or, how we suspect a user would find and use the service.

Ideation

Storyboard

To start off our Ideation Phase, we expanded on the journey of our user and created a visual representation to better help understand the emotions our user could potetionally go through during the process of interacting with Parti.

Valerie needs to plan a birthday party for her son but has very little time to plan and prepare. This causes a lot of stess until she finds out about Parti and is able to schedule rental items as well as purchase things for the party. Since she is able to quickly get everything taken care of straight from her phone, Valerie is able to spend her valuable free time with her family before hosting the party.

Feature Brainstorming & Prioritization

We used the I like, I wish, What if method to brainstorm features that will help solve our user’s problem. This was also important in overcoming scope creep and not making things too complicated.

During our brainstorming session we used Miro to throw ideas up on a 2x2 priority matrix to determine which features we should start creating.

At the top of the list were:

  • Ability to buy party supplies
  • Ability to rent party supplies
  • Party clean-up service

Prototyping

User Flow

Now that we understood our user’s journey, as well as defined our main features, we moved on to creating a User Flow.

Again, this was very necessary to help us keep on track with our main priorties and not run off into areas and features that were not necessary at this time. We originally stuggled with overcomplicating the process, but after receiving outside advice and feedback, we were able to backtrack and simplify the user flow.

Wireframe Sketches

We then took everything that we had researched and planned before and translated those ideas into sketched wireframes. Three different people sketched out their vision and then presented to the whole group for feedback and to decide which design route to go.

We ended up taking a few things from each sketch to create our first digital wireframe.

Digital Wireframes & Prototype v1

Using Figma, I created a digital prototype to conduct usability tests with. These prototype’s were very simple, yet clickable, examples to help us better understand how the process or layout worked.

We did not want to include any images, colors, or creative features that would distract the users we would eventually test from the main goal.

Testing

Usability Testing and Iteration

We started the testing phase by creating a usability testing plan with three tasks for users to complete. We wanted to locate areas of friction and confusion while using the prototype so that we could iterate and improve the user experience.

These tests helped us identify these problems:

  • Confusion around scrolling on the product page
  • Frustration converting military time to regular time when selecting the pickup date on rental items
  • Confusion about the delivery date before adding items to cart

Final Prototype

When analyzing our test results, it was obvious that we needed to make some changes and what they were. We completed updates 1 - 4 on the list, and then did one more usability test which confirmed that the changes were helpful.

The last two usability tests brought up 2 more minor updates that would make expected user behavior a little more clear. This led to updates 5 - 6 on the list:

  1. Added labels to tab menu items
  2. Added slider to indicate scrolling on product page
  3. Changed military time to regular time with AM / PM labels
  4. Grayed out “add to cart” to encourage user to add delivery date first on product page
  5. Changed “rent now” to “checkout now” on product page
  6. Added clarification text to delivery and pickup selector
